What Is a Loan Calculator?

A loan calculator applies amortization mathematics to determine periodic payment amounts required to retire debt over a specified timeframe. It reveals how much interest accumulates and how principal balances diminish with each installment.

Common Uses

  • Comparing monthly affordability across different borrowing amounts
  • Evaluating whether refinancing existing debt would reduce total interest
  • Planning major purchases like vehicles, education, or home improvements
  • Understanding how extra principal payments accelerate payoff timelines
  • Budgeting for consistent monthly outflows over multi-year periods

Keyboard Shortcuts

  • Enter loan amount without dollar signs or commas — the tool formats automatically
  • Specify terms in years; the calculator converts to monthly periods internally
  • Input interest rates as whole numbers (5.5 for 5.5%) rather than decimals
  • The amortization table reveals exactly how each payment splits between principal and interest
  • Export the schedule mentally by noting the monthly payment and total interest figures

Pro Tips

  • Shorter loan terms dramatically reduce total interest paid despite higher monthly obligations
  • Even small extra principal payments in early years yield substantial long-term savings
  • Compare APR versus nominal interest rates when shopping for credit products
  • Biweekly payments (half the monthly amount every two weeks) create an extra full payment annually
  • Refinancing makes sense when current rates drop at least 1-2 percentage points below your existing rate

Frequently Asked Questions

What does amortization mean?

It describes the gradual elimination of debt through regular payments covering both principal and interest over time.

How does the calculator determine monthly payments?

It applies the standard amortization formula: P × [r(1+r)^n] ÷ [(1+r)^n - 1], where P equals principal, r equals monthly rate, and n equals total payments.

Can I see how much goes to interest versus principal?

Yes. The amortization table breaks down every single payment into interest portion and principal reduction.

What happens if I increase my monthly payment?

Higher payments reduce principal faster, shortening the repayment period and decreasing total interest.

Does this include origination fees or insurance?

No. This tool computes principal and interest only. Add taxes, insurance, and fees separately for complete payment estimates.
